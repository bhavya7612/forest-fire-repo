It indicates any fires that start have the potential to become large and uncontrollable. Following things you can do in current situation:
  1. Monitoring and Early Detection:
    • Regularly monitor weather conditions, especially temperature, relative humidity, wind speed, and precipitation.
    • Use reliable weather stations or online resources to stay informed about changing conditions.
    • Set up early detection systems such as fire lookout towers, cameras, or satellite imagery to identify potential fire starts promptly.

  2. Fuel Management:
    • Assess the vegetation in your forest area. Identify areas with high fuel loads (dry grass, dead trees, etc.).
    • Implement fuel reduction strategies, such as controlled burns, mechanical thinning, or creating firebreaks.
    • Maintain defensible space around structures by clearing flammable materials.

  3. Emergency Preparedness:
    • Develop an emergency plan that includes evacuation routes, communication channels, and assembly points.
    • Train staff and volunteers on wildfire response procedures.
    • Coordinate with local fire departments and emergency services.

  4. Public Awareness and Education:
    • Educate residents and visitors about fire safety.
    • Promote responsible behavior (e.g., no campfires during high-risk periods).
    • Share information about fire danger levels and restrictions.

  5. Fire Suppression Resources:
    • Ensure that firefighting equipment (e.g., hoses, pumps, shovels) is readily available.
    • Establish mutual aid agreements with neighboring landowners or agencies.
    • Consider having a dedicated firefighting crew or contracting with a private firefighting service.

  6. Restrictions and Bans:
    • Implement fire restrictions or bans when the FWI is high.
    • Prohibit open burning, fireworks, and other activities that could ignite fires.
